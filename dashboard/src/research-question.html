Research Question

Long-form framing of the NBA supermax stay-versus-move performance question.

The Problem

The NBA gives a team a structural advantage in keeping its own stars that no rival can match. Through Bird rights, a team can re-sign its own player even when doing so pushes the team over the salary cap. Through the supermax, officially the Designated Veteran Player Extension, a qualifying player can start a new contract at 35% of the salary cap, a figure no other team is allowed to offer him.

Behavioral economics points the other way. The fresh-start or reset effect, studied by Hengchen Dai and others, finds that people often raise performance after a temporal or situational reset: a new job, a new beginning, a clean slate. A player who changes teams gets that reset. A player who re-signs at home does not.

That creates a collision between structural and psychological incentives. The money says stay. The fresh-start evidence suggests moving might improve performance. This study asks what actually happens when a star takes the supermax and stays.

The Question

Do players who re-sign with their incumbent team on a supermax underperform contract-equivalent peers who changed teams, after controlling for age and pre-signing performance trajectory?

The core hypothesis is that supermax recipients show the largest negative performance delta in the middle years of the contract relative to expectation, because they receive neither the fresh-start benefit of a move nor the market discipline that competitive offers can impose.

Why This Is an NBA Question

This research extends a prior study that applied the same behavioral economics lens to NHL free agency. The lens transfers, but the question does not. The NHL has no supermax and no mechanism that lets one team pay a player a number every other team is forbidden to match. That NBA-specific rule is the center of this study.

How It Is Measured

Each contract decision is sorted into one of three treatment groups:

  • Re-signed on a supermax: stayed with the incumbent team on a Designated Veteran contract or extension.
  • Re-signed on a standard deal: stayed with the incumbent team on any non-supermax contract.
  • Changed teams: signed with a different team as an unrestricted free agent.

Performance is measured with Box Plus-Minus (BPM), a publicly available impact metric from Basketball Reference that decomposes into offensive (OBPM) and defensive (DBPM) components. This lets the analysis show not just whether a re-signed player declines but where the decline appears.

Contract types are classified with published CBA thresholds, including the 25%, 30%, and 35% maximum-salary tiers by years of service and supermax eligibility rules.

What This Study Does Not Claim

This is a single-builder research project driven by intellectual curiosity, not a predictive product. It does not claim to forecast individual outcomes, advise on roster construction, or replace front-office analysis. It is a transparent, auditable test of whether a well-documented behavioral pattern appears in NBA contract decisions.

Roadmap: Future Questions

  • The James Harden effect: what happens when stars force moves while still under contract, and how should those moves be coded consistently?
  • Mean reversion across all signings: whether high-performing signings regress and low-performing signings rebound.
  • Player-empowerment-era selection effects: whether free-agency entrants are a non-random talent sample because top players increasingly engineer trades.
